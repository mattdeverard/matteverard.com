Lessons for Starting an Agile Library and Creating a Reading Culture in India for Your MNC

By Matt Everard · April 24, 2017

Getting our team on a regular diet of reading was key to generating excitement about a new way of working. Learning how other companies are succeeding creates natural competition. Plus, our folks love ideas and chatting about them. Here are a few tricks to help you. Further down is my list of “must reads” and crowd favourites in Pune

  1. Buy the books yourself — your colleagues might be rich (family land) or scraping by (4 mouths, 1 income) but they will all be frugal. It is (understandably) part of the culture. No worries. You can play librarian. Consider doing monthly expense reports to cover this and other purchases (e.g. post-it notes, markers, LEGO, prizes) as part of your servant-leadership role. As my colleagues said numerous times, “Boss won’t refuse your expenses. But mine…not sure”
  2. Make the bookshelf very visible — I had them stacked next to my desk and shoved them in people’s faces, saying, “Have you read this? IT CHANGED MY LIFE” and then had a story to back it up. You can’t do that with ebooks
  3. Have a signout system— books will disappear into the ether otherwise (as they do in any country but in India your teams are likely so large, your chance of recovering a book without records is nil)
  4. Put a tight time limit on checkouts — I had a 1 week policy that always got stretched to 2, but after 2 wks the likelihood of a book being read drops precipitously
  5. Walk around with your current reading material in hand. Sit in a prominent space and read in public. During work hours. Tell other to do the same. We had a very big exec who regularly told the troops to “invest 30 mins to an hour a day in yourself, your career. You will be surprised how fast it adds up.” I’d always quote him and remind people that he’s telling you to think of your careers in a new way — you are not low-cost factory workers; you are high-value knowledge workers who can compete with any region
  6. Make sure your management team and leaders are always reading something. Ask them, What are you reading this week? If they are stuck, help them find a book and then buy it for them. They will almost certainly read it straight away. In the US this might not work, but Indians take gift-giving very seriously. It’s one of the many awesome things about life here

Books

  • Leading the Transformation, Gruver & Mouser — 104 pages of awesome. Most important book I read. Based on HP Firmware transformation achieving a 10x improvement. I bought over a dozen copies. Handed them out like candy
  • Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work in Half the Time, by the Sutherlands, is a best-seller for good reason. I read it twice and then got the audible version. It’s just that good — punchy, real-world, no bullshit. Handed it out to several execs as well
  • Software in 30 Days, by Schwaber and Sutherland, another great book by the masters, this one is easily consumed by your business peers and sets the rationale for agile transformation in business terms: “agility is an organization’s ability to meet a market opportunity.” Now, who doesn’t want to be agile?
  • Agile IT Agile IT Organization Design: For Digital Transformation and Continuous Delivery, by Sriram Narayan, is a must-read for CXOs looking to reshape their organizations for the 21st c. This tome was passed from leader to leader in our org and fundamentally shaped our TOM strategy
  • Continuous Delivery: Reliable Software Releases through Build, Test, and Deployment Automation, by Jez and David, is a classic and a must-read for our engineering leads
  • Decisive: How to Make Better Choices in Life and Work, by Chip and Dan Heath, will give you teaching material for your troops about decision-making processes, biases, and strategies for improving the culture on the floor. Many big IT shops are stuck in 90’s era thinking. Use this material in some Town Halls and get people talking
  • Switch: How to Change Things When Change Is Hard, by Chip and Dan Heath. Ditto for this one. The Rider, Elephant, and Path metaphor is sticky and will become part of your office parlance
  • Drive: The Surprising Truth About What Motivates Us, by Dan Pink. Autonomy, Mastery, & Purpose are the motivational tools for the creative class. Our leadership team loved this book and the teams loved Dan’s TED talks
  • Lean Enterprise: How High Performance Organizations Innovate at Scale, by Jez, Joanne, and Barry, was written, as Jez says, to show CIOs how to implement what was described in Continuous Delivery
  • The Lean Startup: How Today’s Entrepreneurs Use Continuous Innovation to Create Radically Successful Businesses, by Eric Ries, will introduce a variety of entrepreneurial concepts to your teams — the importance of experimentation, requirements as hypotheses, vanity metrics. My favourite vanity metric in agile transformation — “number of projects running agile.” What is a project? What is agile? What’s our goal here?
  • The DevOps Handbook: How to Create World-Class Agility, Reliability, and Security in Technology Organizations, by Gene, Jez, John, John, and Pat, is a comprehensive handbook that your leadership team and keeners alike will enjoy
  • The Power of Habit: Why We Do What We do in Life and Business, by Duhigg. One IT exec translated the “zero workplace injury” idea from ALCOA into a “zero defect” cornerstone quality program. I thought he was nuts at first, but when I connected the dots, that defects are like injuries — hygiene indicators of workplace sloppiness, poorly engaged workers — I could get on board. The book is chock full of insights, thoughtfully delivered, easily translatable into town halls
  • The Second Machine Age: Work Progress And Prosperity In A Time Of Brilliant Technologies, by Brynjolfsson & McAfee. Just because. It’s fun and full of great ideas. Remember, a big part of your job is to nurture a culture of ideas

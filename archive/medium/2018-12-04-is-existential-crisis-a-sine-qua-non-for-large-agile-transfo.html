Is Existential Crisis a Sine Qua Non for Large Agile Transformation?

By Matt Everard · December 04, 2018

Let’s think of a large agile transformation as very dangerous, complex, experiment, comparable to a Crispr attempt to change the DNA of big dinos into small mammals. It’s not going to be easy. It may not even be possible and surely will require new ways of working, inventions and a culture of discovery coupled with a fanatical collective determination to succeed

“Ahh, you are being too dramatic Mr Everard! We have succeeded in many LeSS and SaFE transformations!” say a small army of well-paid consultants. To wit: I call bullshit. Oh, is that too harsh? Let me explain

Agility is Release Cadence

Sutherland and Schwaber, in Software in 30 Days, give us this:

Agility is a measure of one’s ability to take advantage of an opportunity. We can turn on a dime, immediately launch bold initiatives, and manage our risks. We can make our competitors weep when they wake up in the morning, and we can please our newly found customers

In the hundreds of conversations I’ve had over the “essence of agile,” the smart kids, the folks whose business sponsors buy them flowers and chocolate, insist on focusing on the “one metric that matters” — release cadence. Wait haters — there are other metrics that matter (MTTR, quality, etc), but I’m taking those as a given — hygiene factors. If you can release multiple times a day into production, you have a rock-solid delivery pipeline that doesn’t rely on humans to inspect for quality and has automated roll-backs.

Let’s Examine the Current State of Enterprise IT

Today’s MNC IT shop is

  • Focused on stability over innovation
  • “Just don’t break anything”
  • Terrified of change — most large changes fail

So there are many, many layers of decision makers: exec sponsors, planning committees, spreadsheet jockeys, financial analysts, program managers, all of this to determine what *should be built*

Then there are the requirements, build, and test phases, followed by BAT and release weekend. This is to ensure availability and quality. We need all these phases because we are relying on 90’s tech and manual processes

It’s not uncommon to take 18 months from idea to production in big IT shops

Welcome to the transformation

Globally nearly all large finservs are going through “transformation.” Often “digital” or “agile” or “devops” is prepended. What does this mean?

Your execs know:

  • The company is a dinosaur
  • The meteor (disruption) is coming
  • Only small furry mammals will survive (read: highly experimental, passionate about customer experience, able to respond to change in days not months or years)

This organizational DNA change, dino-to-mammal, is complex in the extreme. I see 3 possible states of being for big bank IT in the next few years:

  1. Still a brachiosaurus — tried to transform but failed. Too painful, too many political problems, visionary leaders move on, middle-management revolts, new way of working creates service quality problems causing the business to revolt. This will be the majority of big banks
  2. Made it to Velociraptor — successfully implemented a scaled agile framework and achieved a 20% improvement in performance/quality from the IT organization. Costs are down considerably compared to peers. Best of class IT organization wins the CIO awards and bonuses. Still has a cost structure way out of whack with a disruptor. Change is much faster than before but idea-to-release is many months, not days
  3. Hair and tits — an “enterprise IT” that looks more like AMZN than a bank. This will require incredible fortitude and leadership as the organization itself is has an immune system built to reject the change. Imagine convincing bankers that the world they’ve known for decades, that has made them rich, is about to explode. That’s not even the hardest part. It’s dismantling the ossified fiefdoms that have evolved to support the current state

I’ve seen some attempts in-progress in the wild, usually some form of “create your own Uber” inside the bank. Build or buy a platform and cannibalize your existing business. These companies, if they can execute, will decimate their competition. It won’t be a fair fight. Fees will be lower, quality of service higher, digital platform offerings far, far superior

Transformation is possible if you partner with the business

I have seen a few examples of true transformation in big bank IT. One group in particular really stands out. 200 engineers across 3 regions who moved from a handful of releases per year to thousands. How’d they do it? They started by enlisting their global MD — a very big dog to have in your fight — which gave them the political capital to fight battles with the lovely souls in change management, risk, security. Key takeaways:

  1. Excellent technical leadership who were so persistent and fearless as to be pugnacious shit-disturbers. They were awesome and were not afraid to fight stupid
  2. Tight partnership with the business. An MD actually attended retros and played along as part of the team. At first I thought having the boss in the retro was (potentially) a bad idea, but after listening to the team, I saw that the MD understood the rules of engagement and wasn’t acting like an old school boss. This made the team feel empowered and increased the trust and transparency
  3. “Challenge the team to deliver more frequently. Everything else will follow,” was the regular refrain of technical team lead. This clean, clear message was key to the team’s cohesion and focus
  4. “You get what you measure” was the mantra of the team — so they kept their eyes on production quality, release cadence, and not a lot else. Yeah, they had other numbers but their heart was in those two

Final thoughts

There are a lot of articles on “Why agile transformations fail” — many of them focus on tactics rather than asking, “Did this company approach the change as existential necessity?” Do the CEO and Board believe the company will cease to exist in a few years if they don’t dramatically reshape in short order? In other words, do they see the meteor in the sky?

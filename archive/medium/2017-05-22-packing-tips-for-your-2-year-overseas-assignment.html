Packing Tips for Your 2-Year Overseas Assignment

By Matt Everard · May 22, 2017

First off, everyone is lying to you about the timeframes for delivery. Everything will take 2–3x longer. What follows is some practical advice for dealing with your precious goods

You’ll likely get four places to put everything you own: suitcases, air shipment, sea shipment, and LTS (long term storage)

Suitcases

  • You’ll get at least two @ 32kg per. That’s a lot of weight. It’s pretty hard to make a bag that heavy with soft goods. Why does the airline give such a large allowance? B/c expats put crazy shit in their Samsonites
  • Lesson: Don’t pack like you normally do with all clothes and shoes. Instead add key items from each room in your house. That’s the easiest way to describe it. So bring that french press if that’s your fancy, kitchen tools you use daily but a hotel suite will not have (manual citrus press, juicer, the Maybach of blenders), enough of your toiletries to last 3 months, office supplies, a mini-printer perhaps (India, where we were, is still very paper-based), your Sonos, airport base station, spices and special condiments that you love, sausages (you laugh, but it’s true, every seasoned expat will have have cured meats in his luggage). Walk around your house and tag items that you use everyday. Pack as many of those as you can. Plan to live out of your suitcases for 90 days. No joke
  • Use duffel bags for half of your allowance — huge carrying capacity and condense to nothing in your new flat
  • As for suitcase clothes/shoes: bring a pair of shoes from each category. Boots, sandals, trekkers, runners, dress, office, sport-specific. I forgot to pack trekkers on the way to India (I’m not going hiking in the monsoon!). Yeah, we went hiking in the monsoon. Also went to China on a man-adventure. Basically ruined a fine pair of Varvatos dress boots b/c there was “no fuckin way I’m buying a pair of trekkers when I already own some”
  • Buy two really nice kitchen knives that come in box. Your mobile self will love you. You won’t see your other kitchen goods for 3 months. Bad knives are synonymous with rental suites and nothing in the food prep world sucks like cutting with a bad blade

Air shipment

Timeline: they tell you 7 days, but it’s 30 days door to door

  • You have some extra rules here depending on country — e.g. when exiting India our shipper looked at our health supplements (e.g. charcoal pills) and asked, “Where’s the Rx?” You reply: These are over-the-counter. “The customs guys won’t know that. This needs to go into Sea container.” Customs in some countries is an entrepreneurial affair, and the inspectors are looking for a little baksheesh. You can always risk it, but it’s not advised
  • You’ll get a weight limit on the air shipment. Like all good westerners we confused more with better and tried to max out the allowance. That was stupid. On both ends of our trip we didn’t use much from the air shipment. We were in temporary housing (800sf apartments) until the sea shipment with our beds arrived, so what are we going to do with that fancy office chair? Best advice I can give is put the rest of the tagged items from your initial house walk-about. Stuff of daily use. Keep the amount small so you can absorb it into your tiny apartment for months 2 and 3. Maybe your kid’s desktop computer and xbox

Sea Shipment — e.g. what do you take and what do leave behind?

Timeline: they tell you 30 days but it’s 90+ door to door

  • We have some really nice couches — custom jobs — and bringing them was a vexing question during the pack. Will they get ruined? Will they fit in our new place (which hasn’t been chosen)? Or will they be an albatross that will limit our apartment selection (b/c they are big). And we have a room-sized Persian rug — do we bring it? Again, the albatross question. We brought both and were very happy we did. They limited our apartment selection (we could have lived closer to work and a smaller place), but it also forced us into a new-build (with no furniture). So my advice is this: pack everything you LOVE, everything that makes your life good. Don’t put it in storage where you may not see it again for a very long time

LTS — AKA furniture purgatory

  • This is for heavy, bulky, half-broken, 8 year-old shit that you should have given away. Items that will, upon opening the crate some years later, make you question your consumerist ways. But your employer will pay for it, so why force the emotional discomfort of getting rid of it. Let your garbage age in a warehouse for years. We all do it. When I ponder how much junk like this is in storage across the US and Canada it blows my mind. My advice: give away anything you can, especially tech that gets better and cheaper every month. I gave my buddy a 42" flat-screen. He put it in his office. It feels good to give things away. A win-win. The only things of value we have in LTS are an antique buffet we feared would break in transit, a KitchenAid stand-mixer and other nice 110v kitchen equipment, photo albums that we should have packed, bikes we should stored w/ friends, and my son’s weight set
  • Lesson: LTS is not setup for you to retrieve anything mid-term. It’s a vault that remains sealed until repat-time. We didn’t realize this and tried (in vain) to get our bikes out during a summer trip home. “Sir, you can maybe come at the end of the week and it will cost $150/per hour while you are in the warehouse to cover the cost of the forklift operators” — where are you located? “Out in the middle of nowhere” (read: rent a truck). We never got those bikes

A Final Storage Option

  • Leave some things with friends or in a personal storage locker in your home country — one of my biggest regrets was to put my bicycles and snowsport gear in LTS. You will come home for visits. Everyone does. And you will repatriate someday and will be in temp housing for 3 months waiting for your sea shipment. During that time you won’t have access to your LTS (they will come one day and unload the entire container at whatever address you provide) and will be living like a tourist in your home city. When you pack, prepare a future care package for your visiting self
  • Lesson: Take cold-weather clothing on your assignment (even if you are going to warm location). You will go on vacation to a cold place in the course of your two years in the tropics. Guarantee it. Do you really want to buy $100 gloves in Pune?

One of the best, completely unintended consequences of this experience was to purge our lives of the cruft of daily living. There are so many good books on this, on pairing down your life, getting rid of anything that doesn’t give you joy to touch, but it’s really, really hard to do. Unless you are forced. But I can tell you that we all loved the streamlined life of fewer possessions. My sons can move around the globe with only a backpack and small suitcase. That’s a lot of freedom

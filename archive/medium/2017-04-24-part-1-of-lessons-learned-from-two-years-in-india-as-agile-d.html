Part 1 of Lessons Learned from Two Years in India as Agile & DevOps Transformation Lead for a Very Big MNC

I’ve just wrapped up two years in India as Agile & DevOps Transformation Lead for one the planet’s biggest banks. I want to share some of the fun lessons and tactics behind agile transformation at scale in my little corner of India. I’m not going into strategy or business engagement yet.

Step 1 : Let’s start with chai and BBQ Nation

  • Your first day will be very social. Count on a two hour lunch and so many chai breaks your belly will hurt. Indians are some of the warmest souls on earth. You are (they will make you feel) more special here than you have ever been. Soak in this sunshine. If you’ve had a couple of abusive years in corporate hell, consider this karmic balance. Chat up the big boss, his (and it’s almost certainly a male although that is changing) managers, technical leads, keeners — especially the keeners as they will be your foot soldiers later. Listen and learn for the first 6–8 weeks. Don’t rock any boats. There’s plenty of time for that later. Understand the pain points and how the sausage really gets made. It will shock you
  • Spend lots of time with the PM community — you don’t know it yet, but they hold the keys to your success. PMs in India, Yami G once told me, are regarded as the “land owners,” and dev teams are the farmers. It helps to have the gentry on your side. Win their trust by listening. They are more keen to go agile than you expect. Many of them want self-organizing teams and see being a SM as good for their CV
  • Big shout-outs here to Pankaj, Alok, and Sushil — my PM brethren who carried the torch for change
  • Build a library of physical books that is very visible. Loan out books freely. Get extra copies. Send the receipts to the big boss. Start a book club or discuss regularly over lunch. Publicly praise those who read. Call them thought-leaders. Generate interest in ideas. Quote the books in your town halls. Hold them up for people to see
  • Extend your network. Get lunch with any big boss. It’s pretty hierarchical here. Having relationships with powerful people pays dividends. Plus, you are exotic — one of the few expats on campus. You are more sexy than normal
  • But you will also want to make a lot of friends outside as well. They will be able to answer all sorts of questions you’ll have over the next two years. I was fortunate enough to meet some A-listers like Naresh Jain (leader of Agile India conferences and AgileFAQs), Yami G, Kamlesh Ravlani (Agile for Growth), and the team at ThoughtWorks including Manoj Ramchandani, Sriram Narayan, and Vishal Bhalerao — all indispensable sounding boards and counselors

Step 2: Plant some seeds

  • Start selling the vision in language that addresses pain points you have discovered. Create a 20 minute “Why, How, What” for agile. Sell it personally in mini-town halls. My audience had zero experience with agile so it was important to keep the message simple and consistent
  • I did 30 min sessions with 15–20 people per. After I did my song and dance, they had 8 mins Q&A and we finished with a challenge to them to come up with answers to the question: “What does agile mean to me?” I know, that sounds pretty stupid. It just happened on the spot. But it was so warmly received and created such positive energy that it stuck. I challenged each group to generate more than the last. They had great quotes like “agile is not new — it’s the way humans have be building things for millenia” and “embrace change, don’t fight it.” After 30 of these sessions, I had a lot of home-grown marketing material to plaster across the walls
  • Agile 1,2,3: I like to move fast but knew we couldn’t just flip a large department overnight. What we could do was try becoming agile in 3 steps. First, every team started doing daily standups. Then team-level agile (water-scrum-fall) followed by “true agile” (where the business owns the backlog)
  • Teams loved the standups. No one was forced to adopt them, they just enjoyed it. For the most part
  • Lesson: make sure your folks understand that they should only attend one standup per day. Case in point: We had managers wanting standups with their directs (how can that be bad?) and then directs having to go to distinct standups for each project they were on. Three standups a day! Horrible. But if you make a blanket “no more than one standup” rule the teams will self-organize and figure out a solution (e.g. Sachin will be my surrogate)
  • Lead by example: The senior management team were the first to adopt standups and they were religious about it. There were good days and bad, but everyone now wonders how they managed to survive in the old system (answer: lots of work fell on a handful of key people the big boss trusted)
  • Choose a goldilocks pilot in parallel to the selling. Something that is not too hot, nor too cold. It needs to be big enough to be a meaningful win but not too big as to be a guaranteed failure. You will be like a mother hen to this project. If it fails, pack your bags
  • Ensure the goldilocks team members are having fun and that the on-shore PO is ecstatic — willing to go on record about what she has experienced. Have team members give testimonials to the floor (i.e. all staff) about what they’ve learned. People will line up to be a part of the next tranche of projects running Scrum
  • Emphasize that agile is a more engaging, empowering, and energizing way to work. When one of my colleagues shouted, “It is more human!” I knew they were getting it

Step 3: Grow the Thing, Carefully

  • If you are like most companies, you are still project/program based and HR and the Target Operating Model haven’t caught up to your agile transformation, so pick a program with a steady funding stream and fight for a ring-fenced, co-located team. Die on this hill, my friend. If you try to scale with transient teams, working on multiple projects simultaneously, physically located across many regions, take a pass — your chance of success is near nil. Have your boss call me if you get any guff
  • Hopefully you now have a cadre of x-functional, co-located, ring-fenced teams working on a single backlog. Now go hire a coach. Get a few. You are going to be too busy to handle this alone
  • Industry standard ratios for coaches are 1 coach per 2 teams (each being a two-pizza team) with a coaching engagement lasting 3 months. Ok, this is a lie. There is no “industry standard,” but in conversations with many a seasoned coach we tended to agree that this was a reasonable compromise between the GO-GO-GO of upper management and the realistic needs of the teams on the ground
  • Take a group of thought-leaders to Agile India or LeanKanbanIndia
  • Train your teams: I’ve seen companies pay for 2-day Scrum Master classes. At $1,200 USD this gets pretty pricey when you are multiplying by hundreds. We chose a more frugal approach and created our own 1-day class. And here I got lucky— I found an internal trainer, Prakash Rananavare, who really understands Scrum. And he’s as good a trainer as I’ve ever met. We worked as a pair for much of the training, with the LEGO4SCRUM game at the end of the day. Damn, those were fun times. We trained 600 people in 6 months. I’m not recommending this. It’s just what we did to keep the ball moving. There was a lot of momentum behind the “new way of working” and people wanted the training. Teams were flipping to Scrum without coaches. We said, “Do it! You don’t need to ask permission!”
  • Yeah. This is a little risky. I know. Shitty agile teams can tarnish the agile rep. Thankfully, I didn’t have to deal with much blowback. Perhaps I just got lucky

Part 2 of Lessons Learned from Two Years in India as Agile & DevOps Transformation Lead for a Very Big MNC

Step 4: Start Your DevOps Journey

  • Scaling agile without DevOps is a nightmare. I was lucky enough to be part of a leadership team that knew this. The reason you have this highly structured waterfall system is to ensure quality in a world of largely manual processes. If you keep all those manual processes (testing, environments, config management, deployment) you will always need all the stage-gating, all those gate keepers, and the Herculean efforts surrounding release weekend. Your x-functional Scrum teams will be pulled apart by the gravity of the release cycle (read Leading the Transformation or watch Jez’ Stop Scaling Agile presentation)
  • Create a DevOps SWAT team to start automation and tool training for the masses — recruit your best keeners who are passionate about this tech and do whatever it takes to keep them happy. They will burn the midnight oil for you if you give them fun work to do. My team went to a week-long bootcamp on CI/CD and Mule. Then returned to teach about 80 engineers what they learned. This gives you the BW to scale your DevOps initiatives.
  • Big shout-out to Shailendra and Sandesh for being leaders in every way — my time in India would have been radically diminished without you two!
  • Note: It was not uncommon to have people working until late in the night. It was not a death march, not compulsory, but part of the hardworking culture. It’s also a function of traffic patterns. If you leave the office before 18:30 you’re good. If you leave anytime btw 18:30–21:30 you will be in gridlock for a long time. My 8km commute could take 1.5 hrs during monsoon and regularly took an hour
  • Recognize this DevOps team in front of their peers and showcase their work when big execs come to town. They are doing plumbing work. It’s not glamorous unless you bring the party. We made a 5 minute video set to Indian dance pop music and broadcast it far and wide. Everyone loved it — execs in our LOB, execs outside our LOB, devs from all regions — that was a lot of fun. This team also presented at internal, massive un-conferences and their booth was one of the most popular
  • Get a “CIO mandate” to do crazy shit, such as automating mainframe deployment. The tools are there to do it. All it takes is vision and commitment…and loads of coordination and communication…but if the CIO is behind it, the rest of IT will come alongside. Note: Your CIO will love your audacious proposal. When you succeed, it’s a nice feather in her cap to show her bosses and the business

Step 5: Ensuring and Extending Change

  • Every expat’s biggest fear is that the day after you leave, things go right back to the old ways. I began working on my succession planning six months into my two-year gig. That meant finding the talent, clearing it with their bosses, begin building community, and then building a Coaches-in-Training program
  • With this group of internal coaches we created cultural learning events that would be repeated weekly and, after a couple of months, led by the internal staff
  • Think Fridays! are 30 minute agile learning games on the floor in the late afternoon. We use a Nepalese prayer bell to get butts out of chairs. It’s a nice bell — handcrafted, purchased by one of our team from a local artisan. This is pretty splashy behaviour for office culture in any country; here it was just absurd. I found that I had to be a bit over-the-top and channel my inner Jimmy Fallon to make these events successful. This freed up the coaches-in-training to imitate me later — it wasn’t pretentious for them to be outrageous, they were, in a sense, mocking me with everyone getting to laugh
  • Lesson: I found my teams to be rather conservative/quiet in public. For instance, it was often hard to get people to speak up when you asked the crowd a question. I took to calling on people by name. That works pretty well. They all have opinions, but there’s a social deference at work that will keep them quiet unless you pull answers from the crowd
  • During one set of workshops I asked the 30 person gathering direct questions such as, “Who saw Ganesh (BIG Boss) speak today? Come on. Raise your hand.” No one raised their hands. I had five workshops and less than 5 people raised their hand. Afterwards I checked with the event leader who said, “Man, we had 90% of the building in attendance for Ganesh’s talk. I think you just experienced a ‘cultural difference!’”
  • YouTube Mondays — these were so much fun, I decided to write another article on this
  • Bi-monthly LEGO4SCRUM game for new joiners — 2.5 hours in the middle of the floor, led by the coaches. In India there’s a lot of turnover. What better way to welcome new teammates?
  • Awards like “Just Do It” that I copied from Amazon
  • Team led floor presentations to show off new DevOps tech or successful (or failed!) agile projects
  • Agile Labs — “block booked” meeting rooms in the middle of the floor for daily standups. No one could book them through Exchange. They had to signup on the whiteboard in the room. 20 minute slots. This gives each team a home with a regular meet time. Note 20 min slots and not 30. Yes. Very intentional
  • Note: Meeting rooms were political territory. They are in short supply and possession of one confers power. The rest of the floor is “open office” design so having privacy = importance. Some local bosses take pride in having the biggest mtg room for any/every meeting. Visiting execs use the second nicest (or the nicest!) as their personal office. Getting a couple of these converted to agile labs was a true coup
  • Agile corners — we had too many standup teams to accommodate in the labs, so we got portable flat screens and whiteboards in the corners and in the cafe to expand our capability

Final Thoughts

  • Only time will tell if my two years of work brought lasting change. Organizational entropy is a real thing. Failure appears to be the norm in big IT agile xforms.
  • But I’m hopeful. The teams I worked with were pretty amazing. Loads of talent. Embracing change. Leadership is all aboard. Fundamental changes to HR roles and TOM are in place. As one exec said, “We burned the boat. There’s no going back.” There’s other reasons I’m hopeful, but I’ll have to write about those later
  • I was invited, last minute and rather haphazardly, to a review of one of our key agile programs in my last month there. I was an afterthought because the teams were operating completely autonomously. They were showing a “before and after our Scrum tune up” picture of the teams’ performance/quality and the numbers were compelling — and legit (there’s a lot of lying in this business). These amazing people were becoming rock-stars with their business sponsors, and I had no part in it. I couldn’t have been happier

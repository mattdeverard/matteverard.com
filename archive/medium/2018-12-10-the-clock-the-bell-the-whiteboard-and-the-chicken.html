The clock, the bell, the whiteboard and the chicken.

By Matt Everard · December 10, 2018

The clock, the bell, the whiteboard and the chicken. How engagement devices help create teams on a liveaboard scuba boat

16 strangers. 14 crew. 12 nationalities. 19 dives. 7 days

Two years ago, I didn’t know such things existed. We were in India at the time, and I was planning a fun spring break trip for my high school aged sons and landed on scuba. We went to the Andamans, the islands 1400 km off the coast of the subcontinent, and did 10 dives in 5 days from land. But from that point, I knew I would have to try a liveabord. This year, I hit my greed limit (i.e. the upper bound of the range you need to live for the year) and decided to stack up a few adventures in SE Asia instead of decorating trees and wrapping gifts

“Scuba is more a meditation than a sport” Jonathan, Captain of The Junk

And a wee bit of a dangerous one — not crazy downhill mountain bike dangerous — but if you fuck up at 30m your spouse will be reading the fine print in your life insurance policy about extreme sports

I’ve never done a cruise. I read A Surprisingly Fun Thing I Will Never Do Again ,and the matter was resolved for me. A liveaboard is the anti-cruise. It’s a working boat with duties for all. The boat isn’t that big, either. We’re talking 31m long for 30 people. And yet it felt adequate, spacious even, due to the high degree of teamwork baked into the system

The boat captain is not a traditional military type. He’s got to fold in some fun-making and hospitality into the role while making sure no one dies. How does he fast-track our teams through the Tuckman model? With a sense of humor, a clock, a bell, a whiteboard, and a chicken

The Clock

There’s only one clock on board, prominently placed in the main cabin. You could see it from every angle. We watched the clock because the clock was god. It told you when you were going to wake up, dive, eat, have a beer. There’s only one way to squeeze four dives in a day: stay on schedule

The Bell

When rung, everyone comes to the cabin. Period. Meals. Dive briefings. Wake up. Person-over-board. Anything important got the bell. Anyone can ring it, but only Bee rings it with style

The Whiteboard

Each day was decomposed here. This was your guide for the next 24 hours. No one need ask “When are we diving next?” If they did, the captain would utter no words and just point to the board

The Chicken

People make mistakes. You forget your mask, and the dive dinghy has to return to the main ship to get it for you, delaying your team. Most of these mistakes are self-inflicted. A lack of attention paid to your process for getting ready. Too much chit-chat with your buddy. Whatever. If this was you, you got the chicken attached to your tank for the next dive. Anyone could assign the chicken to another diver. This kept us on our toes. No one wanted the chicken. And no one got it. It hung prominently next to the wetsuits, taunting us all. We spoke of things as “chicken-able offenses.” We stayed alert at the most critical time of the dive — the lead-up to when you will be underwater

Engagement Devices Form Scaffolding for New Teams

Each of these are:

  • low tech and low cost — easily replaceable and require no training
  • simple — did not require a manual to use or understand
  • highly “visible” — ask any of the divers years from now where the bell was located and they will be able to tell you
  • transcend potential language barriers — international teams need as little reliance on one language as possible (yeah, the board uses words, but it’s hard to misunderstand “dive briefing”)
  • playful — the clock developed a persona, the bell ringing was done with a dance, the chicken is a chicken

Office Culture is Nearly Devoid of Engagement Devices

This point needs no belabouring. One clear benefit of the agile movement is a focus on humanizing the workplace. People are not resources. Meetings need to be interactive. Learn by doing. Play is not a four-letter word. When you use engagement devices in your office teams, e.g. tossing a ball to move the conversation, ringing bells for demos, funny hats, you see improvements

Do yourself a favor — go shop for an engagement device for your team this weekend and see what your team does with it

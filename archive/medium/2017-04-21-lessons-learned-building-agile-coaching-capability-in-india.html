Lessons Learned: Building Agile Coaching Capability in India

By Matt Everard · April 21, 2017

Did you know that if you have been a Scrum Master or even a member of a Scrum team in India for just one project, you can throw “Coach” on your CV and get a 50% pay bump? It’s true. And if you are going to hire some good coaches in India, I have some lessons for you

  1. There are 3 levels of coaches: shit, junior, and expert. Assuming you can weed out the first, you will find junior coaches (couple of years of agile coaching experience, 5 years scrum, CSP) through large GSVs such as Wipro and CapGem. They cost ~$450 USD per day. They range from poor to decent to great at working with local teams, leading agile learning events, and doing assessments. It took me 3 months to find my first good junior. She turned out to be a rock-star who organized the rest of the coaches while I dealt with senior management. Lesson: Don’t underestimate your “juniors” — I found several of them who were fantastic
  2. The experts start at $1,000/day. That’s not a typo — that’s market rate. And that’s for independents. Go to a large global agile consultancy and you will pay $1,800/day. Yeah. No one in London believed me either. They told me I should quit and go get one of those gigs. “I’m thinking about it.” You’ll want at least one expert coach on your team to deal with senior management and on-shore leadership
  3. You will have a hard time hiring that expert. You can get approval from, say the Global CIO in writing, but the local guy who pulls the trigger on the contract will balk. His boss will kill him if he brings in someone this expensive. CIOs come and go, but this guy and his boss are survivors, and survivors don’t spend money like this
  4. Expect to wait 3 months to on-board new coaches if they are currently employed elsewhere. That’s pretty standard math in India. BGV takes a while and standard notice periods are crazy long
  5. Expect your really good, junior coaches to leave you in 6 months. They will have great job offers in Europe or big promotions in-country. You will not be able to compete
  6. Get started very early on creating an internal coach-in-training (CIT) program. You have no idea when someone “upstairs” will cut your budget nor can you plan to keep those great junior coaches
  7. I’d recommend a sort of Shu-Ha-Ri internal coaching curriculum. About 6–12 months for each stage. In addition to active coaching of new teams, each stage has a list of activities/achievements such as leading LEGO games, hosting learning events, facilitating team self-assessments, hosting a Town Hall or debate on an agile topic, attending an industry conference such as Agile India. This gives the internal staff a longer term vision and avoids the tendency to dumb-down the expectations for “internal certifications.” I want our internal coaches to be able to walk across the street and get a legit coaching gig at Barclays or IBM
  8. Note: Getting approval to go to a conference is a big deal here — bosses tend to dismiss them as boondoggles and the old “low-cost factory” mentality is alive and well in many managers — so putting confs in the curriculum forces management to approve the travel request — smart, eh?

Getting a team of great coaches in place is crucial for transformation. They bring a skillset that is probably in very short supply among your team, and they bring an outsider’s perspective. Your team needs to see the “new way of working” modelled on a daily basis. Having coaches also brings a great deal of energy to the transformation. The teams know that senior management is serious — they are spending money! — and this helps the staff take risks and try new things. I am so grateful for my coaching team — without them, I would have been hooped. They were caring, thoughtful, articulate, well-read, hard-working professionals whom I consider friends. Thanks team for all your hard work

Lesson Learned: Get the CIT program goals into the aspiring coaches’ HR performance management plan. I don’t know how you manage your people, but in a very large MNC (and especially in India), the perf mgmt doc and your annual review has more power than you and your fancy talk. A lot more. When push-comes to shove, your CIT program will suffer when their line manager’s pet project needs manpower

Q: Tell me about a time when you were thrown into a new situation where you didn't know anything about the domain. What did you do?

There are a number of stories from my career that pertain (off-shore development, outsourcing a data center, and managing third-party vendors all come to mind), but the one I'm most proud of has to do with grad school.

I was an EE undergrad and the curriculum did not allow much time for creative course work (big surprise!)--lots in the way of eMag, circuits, and digital processing, but very little humanities. I went to grad school to broaden my horizons, and the program I selected required I learn ancient Greek. The traditional way to deal with this insufficiency is to enroll in the summer crash course and cover two years of material in 10 weeks. A former professor and mentor convinced me otherwise. He pointed out that you wanted to love the language and savor the experience over a period of months, not weeks. I agreed, bought a few books, and on my lunch hours leading up to matriculation, learned the language on my own. I figured I still could benefit from summer school and enrolled. After two classes, the professor told me to save my money and go to the library to complete my self-study. At the end of the summer, I tested into the top band of new students (and saved many thousands of dollars).

Although not a business-world example, it demonstrates how I deal with new challenges. I research "best practices" while trying to find cost-effective alternatives to meet my specific situation. I take a low-risk approach (I did show up at summer school, after all) but find "out of the box" solutions that will deliver uncommon results in a timely, efficient manner.

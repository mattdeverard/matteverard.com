Q: "How do you communicate?"


Quite frankly, this is one of the worst questions I have ever heard in an interview, but believe it or not, I've had it asked a half dozen times. The reasons I find this profoundly inane are:

1. You are speaking with me right now. Can't you tell?
2. Communication is complex subject. Can you give me a context? Is this a sales situation? Are we in the middle of a project and we've discovered a problem? Have we known each for a day or a year? With whom am I communicating? Is it to an individual or a group? Is it a peer or a superior?

Communication is 80% of the PM's job and it's fair to ask questions that investigate my skills and approach, but if you want to get at some meat, you have to work a bit harder.

In new environments I seek to understand how my team members operate and adopt my style accordingly. I've had architects and developers who absolutely hated meetings. They wanted all communication asynchronously (as they said), meaning emails. This enabled them to stay in their work flow for a large part of the day and only answer questions during slotted times. I've had executives who absolutely don't read email. They get them, sure, but they don't have time to digest them (which becomes obvious after a couple of days on the job). In those cases, I set up regular time to meet in person with a bulleted list of issues, risks, recent developments, etc. In 30 minutes we cover all relevant topics and come to conclusions about strategic next steps. I've had peers who want a boatload of documentation "tossed over the wall" and those who would rather discuss and jointly develop a meaningful set of requirements (can you tell which I prefer?). In every case, I work in context to move the project forward.

The only thing I won't do is adopt a communication method that will get us into trouble. For instance, if an exec is extremely busy and distracted and is not showing up for meetings or doesn't appear to have taken ownership of the project, I will stop the project. I will not continue filling out status reports and hope for the best. It's not fair to the developers nor is it fair to the exec because they are spending money for something I know will be a waste. Once the project is stopped, the exec will come to terms with their schedule and take ownership or, as sometimes happens, realize that this is not the right time for this project. Pretending that we'll have a happy ending when the exec isn't in control is a recipe for disaster, and life's too short to work on bad projects.

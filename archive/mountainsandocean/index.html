Preventing Innovation

Scott Sehlhorst gives us an irreverant, entertaining playbook for preventing innovation. He begins:

At a recent presentation in Austin by Seilevel about the goals and methods of requirements gathering, a member of the audience asked “What can we do with our requirements to assure innovation?” That’s a tough question with an easy answer - nothing.

What if the question had been “What can we do to prevent innovation?” That’s a better question with a lot of answers.


Of particular interest:
5. Treat employees like garbage. Yell at them. Whenever possible, call them at midnight to yell at them some more. They work for us. If they get uppity, make them work on the weekends. Make them dig holes and fill them back up again. Threaten them - especially when they need the job. If you can’t yell, at least be condescending in public forums. Remember we are smarter than they are. Punks.
Reminds me of the Peopleware chapter on "making teams jell" (short answer: you can't).

An Open Letter to the Chairman of Audible.com

I sent this email a few weeks back and have not heard a reply. Perhaps it was too cheeky--this was after a very frustrating visit to the site. Let me know if you are an Audible subscriber with similar complaints.

Dear Mr Katz,

I have been an Audible customer for several years and frequently evangelize your services to friends and colleagues. Recently my "customer experience" has been going down the toilet, and I'm starting to loathe visits to your site. I write this email in the hope you will make the necessary changes before people like me defect in droves.

1. I can't find what I want. Your (new) merchandising layout is awful. I don't say this kind of thing lightly (I know how hard it is to strike a balance between marketing needs and clean design). I put up with your old layout (which was lacking but serviceable), but this newer version has these fatal flaws:

a. Home page real estate never has anything for me. Over 50% of the space is dedicated to huge spotlights (today Ricky Gervais and Dan Brown? No thanks. I read Mr Brown a while ago and Gervais may be interesting, but I'm a monthly subscriber and not interested in getting out my wallet). Key fact: I'm spending 2-3x as much time trying to locate a new book as before. Perhaps you've noticed that your average customer visit time has increased? Don't take that as good news.

b. The left-hand categories are too numerous (humans can manage 5-8 well and maybe up to a dozen but you have over 30!) and, in my case, they are irrelevant. Do I want a bio or a biz book today? I don't know. I want to listen to something good. My interests are eclectic (last month The Wisdom of Crowds but in January it was The Year of Magical Thinking). Why can't you show me books that have high review scores from readers and critics?

c. Your "New Releases/Our Top Sellers/In the News" area is a nice idea poorly executed. You can't read the titles of most of the books b/c of space (e.g. a featured product reads "What Price L"?), can't see any blurb about the book without clicking through (take a look at Netflix for an elegant solution to this type of problem), and we only get <12 recs, many of which are really old.

d. The top categories "Best Sellers/Award Winners" are at times usable, but most of the inventory is old--is there a shortage of quality product on the market? The top "New Releases" category is also unhelpful--today a long list of $1 items from the Biography station. I am a monthly listener--I want to find another BOOK!

e. Who came up with the left hand navigation elements such as "I want to: feel like a kid again"? Maybe some focus group liked that approach, but I think it stinks. I want to find something good. So much of what is out in the market is rubbish, and I want you to help me avoid it.

2. Major usability issues. I get "This American Life" every month. Why not let me sign-up for an automatic renewal? Last monthy it took me ten minutes to find TAL and buy it. I wanted to upgrade a few months ago to get two periodicals per month, but your site wouldn't let me upgrade my basic listener program until my year was over! I got a new computer and your DRM software said I had too many devices. On top of this, everything loads very slooooowly. All user actions require a page reload?!! Where is the AJAX? Is BroadVision the problem? If so, dump it now.

3. Summary: it feels like the VP of marketing runs the site. He's always trying to sell me something and he's doing a bad job. Mr Chairman, if I were you, I'd put an ombudsman in charge of the site, someone who is sympathetic to the customer's needs. You are running a community (at least for monthly subscribers). It needs to feel like a comfortable, smart place that is solving my needs for intellectual content collection and delivery.

With all respect,

Q: Tell me about a time when you were thrown into a new situation where you didn't know anything about the domain. What did you do?

There are a number of stories from my career that pertain (off-shore development, outsourcing a data center, and managing third-party vendors all come to mind), but the one I'm most proud of has to do with grad school.

I was an EE undergrad and the curriculum did not allow much time for creative course work (big surprise!)--lots in the way of eMag, circuits, and digital processing, but very little humanities. I went to grad school to broaden my horizons, and the program I selected required I learn ancient Greek. The traditional way to deal with this insufficiency is to enroll in the summer crash course and cover two years of material in 10 weeks. A former professor and mentor convinced me otherwise. He pointed out that you wanted to love the language and savor the experience over a period of months, not weeks. I agreed, bought a few books, and on my lunch hours leading up to matriculation, learned the language on my own. I figured I still could benefit from summer school and enrolled. After two classes, the professor told me to save my money and go to the library to complete my self-study. At the end of the summer, I tested into the top band of new students (and saved many thousands of dollars).

Although not a business-world example, it demonstrates how I deal with new challenges. I research "best practices" while trying to find cost-effective alternatives to meet my specific situation. I take a low-risk approach (I did show up at summer school, after all) but find "out of the box" solutions that will deliver uncommon results in a timely, efficient manner.

My almost eZ life

Borris Mann from Bryght recently put me in contact with Zak Greant of eZ systems North America. I had never heard of eZ (an OSS Content Management System based in Norway) but after a short talk with Zak and some product research, I was intrigued. They have a fairly mature product in an evolving space, a good management team that has refused VC money, and are growing organically--all things I love in a software product company. Zak set up a series of interviews (6 to be precise) including a call with Aleksander, the CEO, in Norway. After each interview I grew more interested. What I really liked was the people--extremely creative polymaths with a lot of drive and attitude to boot.

We looked like we were moving toward a deal, but they had a turn in direction and had to put me on hold. It was great to meet the eZ crew, and I hope to be able to cook dinner for Zak once Jenni and I make our home in Vanc.

Q: "What historical forces enabled the Agile movement to come about?"

This was hands-down one of the best interview questions I've been asked. It was, not surprisingly, at ThoughtWorks in Chicago, the leading app dev consulting house in world. There is no right answer to this question, but it enables a conversation about the industry and will test how deeply I think about my profession. It'll also show whether or not I read.

Olivier and I went on for a few minutes about the change in software project size over time--from large military projects costing hundreds of millions in the 70s and 80s to smaller systems with smaller budgets in the 90s and the different ways you find success in each. We talked about the development of OO languages and improved tools (e.g. ANT, CruiseControl) that allowed for daily builds and TDD (test driven development). I could tell I was in the presence of a software philosopher and I loved it.

I learned something that day: interviews can be fun. I really loved those guys at ThoughtWorks, but in the end I had to turn down their offer. They wouldn't budge on the travel commitment (80%--yikes!), and I was getting the Vancouver fever.

Q: "Why should Microsoft hire you?"

Believe it or not, that was the first question Kashur from MSFT asked me on the phone. Now you think, "Well, at least he's getting to the point quickly." But I hadn't even asked MSFT to hire me. They called me and asked for an interview. I told them I wasn't sure and didn't really see myself there (I had turned down MCS last year). The recruiter told me to give it a try, and I thought, "Well, Redmond is close to Vancouver." I've got a buddy there who enjoys working for the Beast, so I tried to keep an open mind.

Needless to say, I expected a bit more wooing than Kashur offered and his question was a bit of punch in the gut. "I'm not sure how to answer that," I responded. I was dumbstruct. He voilated the number one rule: make sure the other person feels comfortable, especially at the beginning. If you need to get confrontational later you can always go in that direction. But if you stiffle a candidate early, you might never find out what they have to offer.

Kashur felt rushed, and it seemed like the recruiter hadn't briefed him on the situation. She probably dumped a pile of resumes on his desk, and he was struggling through them. I took that as a clue about the culture and withdrew my candidacy after hanging up.

Q: "Tell me a little about yourself"

A: I've recently read a story to my kids from The Magic Treehouse series where Jack and Annie, brother and sister, find (what else?) a magic treehouse that transports them back in time so that they can save the day over and over again. In The Night of the Magician Jack and Annie meet up with four of the "new magicians" at the World's Fair in Paris, 1889, at the top of the Eiffel Tower. The kids have to discover the "new magicians" secrets before an evil magician comes to steal away their knowledge. When Louis Pastuer, Alexander Graham Bell, Thomas Edison, and Eiffel are asked "What is your secret magic?" they scratch their heads and respond.

Eiffel: My secret is that I have a taste for adventure and a love of work and responsibility.
Pastuer: Chance favors the prepared mind.
Bell: When one door closes, another opens.
Edison: Genius is 1% inspiration and 99% perspiration.

After each man gives their "secret" the others nod and clap in appreciation. I found myself resonating with each of these perspectives, perhaps especially Eiffel's. I love challenging projects, love to learn, and have a passion to build things that matter.

As Joel Spolsky said, there are two things that matter most when evaluating a candidate: aptitude and passion. People with aptitude can learn new things and are immensly valuable in a host of situations. People with passion get things done. You want to hire someone who gets things done. I've always felt at home in a company that will give me the ball and allow me to run.

Q: "So tell me how did you decide to move to Vancouver"

In order to answer that one well, I have to take you on a little journey. Are you ready? I'll try to keep it brief.

After grad school in Chicago, I came back to Columbus to be close to family (raising babies = lots of work) and start a company. I can tell you lots of stories about the kids later. Right now, I'll stick to the business.

In 1997 I co-founded a software development company with a buddy from KPMG. He managed the money, and I drove the tech side of the house. We grew our business to over $1M in revenue in less than 3 years, serving volatile flame-outs like Restaurant.com, the more pedestrian BN.com, and the rock solid, "always pays their bills" clients like The Kroger Co. We had a great time along the way and built a team of passionate, talented people. We kept our team small, maintained a family-friendly environment, and resisted the insanity prevalent in the late 90s.

That helped us survive the dot-bomb shakeout, and the company is still a going concern. Unfortunately starting in 2004, the fun project work dried up and our team had to be parceled out to sit at customer sites 5 days a week. That was the beginning of the end for me. Don't get me wrong--it's a profitable business, but all the fun had gone. It was time for me to make a change. My partner was very generous with me, giving me lots of time to figure out what to do next. I ended up going on a long vacation to New Zealand with my wife and sons. What better way to live out your severance than on the beach?

When I returned to Columbus, it was the middle of winter and it was cold and ugly. Coming off the plane I knew what had to be done--we had to move to the coast. At that time I thought it would be NYC--I love the energy and international culture of the Big Apple. Plus, I love brownstones, public transportation, carryout, and liquor stores that stay open until all hours of the night.

A few months later I was skiing at Kicking Horse in the BC interior, and gazing out over the peaks exclaimed, "Damn, I really love the mountains!" My good friends on the trip (who live in Vancouver) sensed my weakness and began an all-out marketing assult. It took a few months, but now my wife and I are sold. The kids are sold. The house is rented. Hell, I've even turned down job offers in NYC (still stings a little).

I decided to do short-term consulting in Columbus as a Program/Project Manager and network my way into a job in Vancouver. If you are reading this, you are proof that my marketing genius is working.

Drop me a note. I love meeting new people.

Q: "Do you like to play Texas hole 'em? "

I'll have to admit, this one caught me by surprise. I hadn't been asked that question before in an interview but, upon reflection, think it is a good one. Just about anyone can say "yes" to this. It is a good ice breaker that can lead to a story about taking home a big pot or losing your shirt to some buddies. Plus, if the person can't say "yes" or doesn't know what "hold 'em" is, you've learned something about them.

These types of culture questions help both parties understand the social style and personality of someone you are going to be hand-in-hand with for the next few years. Turns out this guy, David from eZ, had a few more curve balls.

Later in the interview he asked, "Are you an early bird or a night owl?" I have never been asked that question and I didn't know what to say. Hell, in the States I'm not even sure if you can ask about it--we have many, many "no no's" regarding personal questions. I say, "Night owl." Oops. Turned out to be the wrong answer since 75% of their business is in the Eastern time zone. David was kind enough to tell me that was a concern, and I was able to clarify--I'm a night owl these days, but was an early bird in Chicago where I would bound out of bed at 6am everyday. "I really enjoy the morning," I assure him.

"So what I'm hearing you say is that it won't be a problem to be in the office by 7am everyday?"

Absolutely not.

Q: "How do you go about building a team?"

Got asked this recently by Jennifer at eZ. It's a simple enough question but, boy this is a complicated one to answer. After choking out a less than stellar response, I sent her the following:

Hi Jen, thanks for taking the time to interview me last Thursday. I thoroughly enjoyed your questions and appreciated your honest assessment of the challenges in front of eZ.

I thought a bit over the weekend about questions that I might not have answered thoroughly. Your question about how I would build a team seems very important, and I felt like I should provide more detail.

I recently read Peopleware --a classic you've probably enjoyed--and believe it captures the essence of technology team building: if you hire smart people, treat them with respect, give them responsibility and strategic direction, then your team will be very happy and productive. Engineers enjoy building things that work more than anything else, and they know software dev is complicated. In order to build quality products they have to be able to get into the flow which requires time and a bit of isolation.

This means I limit their attendance at meetings, help them identify and eliminate "time bandits" (e.g business users who call them directly or telephone surveys for Computer World), use my connections to get them involved in industry events, and help them identify pathways for career growth. I've found that if done tactfully, everyone involved is happy (except the magazine companies!).

Having been an engineer myself I'm rather passionate about this aspect of the job. I want my people to relish coming to work and know they are in an environment that encourages them to become master craftsmen. If there are any other questions that I left incomplete, please don't hesitate to drop me a line.

Q: "How do you communicate?"


Quite frankly, this is one of the worst questions I have ever heard in an interview, but believe it or not, I've had it asked a half dozen times. The reasons I find this profoundly inane are:

1. You are speaking with me right now. Can't you tell?
2. Communication is complex subject. Can you give me a context? Is this a sales situation? Are we in the middle of a project and we've discovered a problem? Have we known each for a day or a year? With whom am I communicating? Is it to an individual or a group? Is it a peer or a superior?

Communication is 80% of the PM's job and it's fair to ask questions that investigate my skills and approach, but if you want to get at some meat, you have to work a bit harder.

In new environments I seek to understand how my team members operate and adopt my style accordingly. I've had architects and developers who absolutely hated meetings. They wanted all communication asynchronously (as they said), meaning emails. This enabled them to stay in their work flow for a large part of the day and only answer questions during slotted times. I've had executives who absolutely don't read email. They get them, sure, but they don't have time to digest them (which becomes obvious after a couple of days on the job). In those cases, I set up regular time to meet in person with a bulleted list of issues, risks, recent developments, etc. In 30 minutes we cover all relevant topics and come to conclusions about strategic next steps. I've had peers who want a boatload of documentation "tossed over the wall" and those who would rather discuss and jointly develop a meaningful set of requirements (can you tell which I prefer?). In every case, I work in context to move the project forward.

The only thing I won't do is adopt a communication method that will get us into trouble. For instance, if an exec is extremely busy and distracted and is not showing up for meetings or doesn't appear to have taken ownership of the project, I will stop the project. I will not continue filling out status reports and hope for the best. It's not fair to the developers nor is it fair to the exec because they are spending money for something I know will be a waste. Once the project is stopped, the exec will come to terms with their schedule and take ownership or, as sometimes happens, realize that this is not the right time for this project. Pretending that we'll have a happy ending when the exec isn't in control is a recipe for disaster, and life's too short to work on bad projects.

Q: "How do you handle difficult people?"

I have always wanted to say, "Well, I've been married for 14 years..." and then just stop and smile, awaiting the interviewer's response. Alas, interviews are not stand-up comedy.

Managing conflict is a large part of the PM's job and an interviewer should always explore a candidate's ability to "get to yes." I believe each person is a rational actor and their behavior is driven by a set of assumptions, needs, and fears. If I can understand what motivates them, I can communicate effectively and design win-win situations.

Most project conflict stems from miscommunication surrounding requirements, timelines, and expectations. "Difficult people" are often merely "worried people." They're concerned that they will be held responsible for something that they cannot control or they are already incredibly busy and cannot imagine how they will have time for this project. Once I explain how we've designed the project to not weigh them down and how the lines of responsibility work, "difficult people" are often my best resources. They are grateful that someone has taken them seriously and has addressed their concerns. I have won their loyalty and cooperation on the project.

There are occasional conflicts due to resource contention or politics, but I've found that it is far more rare than water-cooler conversations would suggest.
